## Theme patterns

1. **Role- and purpose-specific access is more granular than a tier.** IHCIA and RCW 70.02 grant or require access for epidemiology centers, Tribal prosecutors, tribal courts, Tribal health providers, and tribal detention personnel only under defined roles, purposes, triggers, and record scopes. TSDF can restrict handling but does not encode those statutory entitlements and predicates.
2. **Consent and continuing control remain incompletely represented.** NIH guidance, the Tribal Resilience Action Database policy, and the USIDSN brief call for early engagement, community-specific governance, documented sharing decisions, secondary-use limits, and in one case continuing opt-out and removal. These do not reduce to a T2/T3 assignment.
3. **Federal-records law creates an explicit boundary.** USGS guidance and Survey Manual 500.6 distinguish internal release policy from FOIA, require honest precollection disclosure of that limit, and preserve written Tribal permission for specified federal and non-federal sharing. A tier cannot promise a FOIA exemption.
4. **Program reporting can be separated from underlying data.** EPA's Exchange Network FAQ requires application, progress, closeout, and final-report information while expressly stating that the program does not currently require a Tribe to send its actual data or specific code outside the Tribe.
5. **Government-to-government process is a separate control plane.** RCW 43.376 imposes collaboration, consultation-process, liaison, communication, training, meeting, and reporting duties. These are `not-represented` in TSDF because they concern governance relationships rather than artifact classification.
6. **Public posting does not equal downstream permission.** The Tribal Resilience Action Database describes outreach and withdrawal despite the underlying plans already being public, reinforcing the collection charter's consent distinction.
